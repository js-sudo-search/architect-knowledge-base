C05 - Non-Functional Requirements

Chapter Objective

Understand why Non-Functional Requirements (NFRs) exist, how they are derived from business objectives, and why they drive architectural decisions.

1. Functional vs Non-Functional Requirements

Functional Requirements define what the system should do. NFRs define how well it should perform that capability.

2. NFR Categories

  • Availability
  • Latency
  • Reliability
  • Durability
  • Security
  • Scalability
  • Observability
  • Maintainability

3. NFRs are Derived, not Selected

Rather than memorizing a checklist, architects ask questions such as:

  • How fast?
  • How available?
  • Can data be lost?
  • What happens during failure?
  • Who can access it?

5. Trade-offs

  • Higher availability generally increases cost.
  • Higher durability may increase latency.
  • Higher consistency may reduce availability.
  • Greater reliability increases operational complexity.

6. Evidence Before Architecture

Case study: A proposal to add Redis was challenged until latency was profiled.

Client + Network : 2.2 s
Backend          : 0.5 s
Payment          : 0.2 s
Database         : 0.1 s

Conclusion: Localize the bottleneck before optimizing.

Distilled Principle

Architecture follows business expectations. Business expectations become Non-Functional Requirements, and NFRs drive architectural decisions.
